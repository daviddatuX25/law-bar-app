# Political Law — Shapes, Trigger Words, Flashcards
Generated from 1987 Philippine Constitution and RA 7160 (LGC)

---

CARD poli-1
FRONT (shape): A person claims that a law or executive action violates a fundamental right guaranteed by the Constitution.
FRONT (trigger words): fundamental rights, Bill of Rights, constitutional violation, due process, equal protection
BACK (provision): Art. III - Bill of Rights (1987 Constitution)
BACK (elements):
1. There is a law, ordinance, or government action
2. The act infringes on a right protected under Art. III
3. The right violated may be: (a) due process (Sec. 1), (b) equal protection (Sec. 1), (c) freedom of speech/expression (Sec. 4), (d) religion (Sec. 5), (e) liberty of abode (Sec. 6), (f) right to information (Sec. 7), (g) right to association (Sec. 8)
4. Government action is the cause of the violation
5. The claimant has standing to raise the constitutional question
BACK (common confusion): Police Power vs. Due Process :: A valid exercise of police power may restrict individual rights. The distinction is whether the restriction is reasonable and in the interest of public welfare (police power valid) or arbitrary and oppressive (due process violation).
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. III; Bar 2020 Q2

CARD poli-2
FRONT (shape): The President issues an executive order, proclamation, or memorandum that exceeds the scope of executive power or encroaches on legislative authority.
FRONT (trigger words): executive power, presidential issuance, executive order, ultra vires, separation of powers
BACK (provision): Art. VII Secs. 1, 17-20 - Executive Department Powers
BACK (elements):
1. The President or an executive official issues an order, proclamation, or directive
2. The issuance must be within the scope of executive power conferred by the Constitution
3. The issuance must not encroach on legislative or judicial powers
4. The President has the power of general supervision over local governments (not control)
5. The President has the power to appoint, control executive departments, and ensure laws are faithfully executed
BACK (common confusion): Art. VI Sec. 1 (Legislative Power) :: The President cannot create laws — that is Congress' power. Executive orders must be within the framework of existing laws. An executive order that creates a new crime or tax without legislative authorization is ultra vires.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. VII; Bar 2019 Q6

CARD poli-3
FRONT (shape): Congress enacts a law and the President vetoes it, or the Congress overrides the veto.
FRONT (trigger words): veto, bill, enactment, override, Congress, President, legislative process
BACK (provision): Art. VI Secs. 26-27 - Legislative Process and Veto Power
BACK (elements):
1. A bill is passed by both Houses of Congress
2. The bill is presented to the President for approval
3. The President signs the bill → it becomes law
4. If the President vetoes, the bill returns to the House of origin with objections (Art. VI Sec. 27 par. 1)
5. Congress may override the veto by 2/3 vote of each House
6. If the President does not act within 30 days, the bill becomes law without signature
7. The President cannot veto a line item in appropriations bills except specific item veto on appropriations, revenue, and tariff bills
BACK (common confusion): Pocket Veto vs. Item Veto :: Pocket veto occurs when the President fails to act within 30 days while Congress is adjourned. Item veto (line-item veto) only applies to appropriations, revenue, and tariff bills — the President cannot veto individual items in other bills.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. VI Secs. 26-27; Bar 2021 Q3

CARD poli-4
FRONT (shape): A private individual or entity exercises governmental functions through a franchise or permit granted by the state, regulated by public utility rules.
FRONT (trigger words): public utility, franchise, common carrier, public service, regulation, CPC
BACK (provision): Art. XII Sec. 11 - Public Utilities and Franchises
BACK (elements):
1. The entity operates a public utility (e.g. transportation, telecommunications, water, electricity)
2. A franchise, certificate of public convenience, or similar authorization is required
3. The franchise must be granted by Congress or authorized legislative body
4. The franchise period cannot exceed 50 years
5. The grantee must be a Filipino citizen or corporation with at least 60% Filipino ownership
BACK (common confusion): Public Utility vs. Public Service :: Not all public services are public utilities. Under the Public Service Act (CA 146, as amended), a "public service" includes a wider range. "Public utility" is a subset that includes only businesses affected with public interest requiring a franchise.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. XII Sec. 11; Bar 2018 Q4

CARD poli-5
FRONT (shape): The State expropriates private property for public use, with payment of just compensation.
FRONT (trigger words): eminent domain, expropriation, just compensation, public use, taking, private property
BACK (provision): Art. III Sec. 9, Art. XII Sec. 18 - Eminent Domain
BACK (elements):
1. There is a taking of private property
2. The taking is for public use or public purpose
3. Just compensation is paid to the property owner
4. The taking is under the authority of law or by government instrumentality
5. For LGUs: ordinance required, authorized by LGC Sec. 19
BACK (common confusion): Police Power vs. Eminent Domain :: Police power regulates property without compensation when necessary for public welfare (e.g. zoning). Eminent domain requires just compensation because it takes property, not merely regulates its use. The distinction: police power destroys/restricts value; eminent domain transfers title.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. III Sec. 9, Art. XII Sec. 18

CARD poli-6
FRONT (shape): A municipality or city enacts an ordinance that conflicts with a national law or exceeds the powers granted to local governments.
FRONT (trigger words): local ordinance, ultra vires LGU, conflict with national law, municipal legislation, delegated power
BACK (provision): RA 7160 Secs. 16, 22, 48-49 - Local Government Powers and Limitations
BACK (elements):
1. The LGU (province, city, municipality, barangay) enacts an ordinance
2. The ordinance must be within the scope of powers delegated by the LGC
3. The ordinance must not conflict with the Constitution or any national law
4. The ordinance must not impose taxes already imposed by national law (unless expressly allowed)
5. The ordinance takes effect after approval by the sanggunian and publication/postering
BACK (common confusion): General Welfare Clause (Sec. 16) vs. Specific Powers :: The General Welfare Clause is a residual power — it allows LGUs to act for general welfare even if no specific power is granted. But it cannot override a clear national law prohibition. Specific powers (tax, police, eminent domain) must be expressly conferred.
SOURCE: RA 7160 Secs. 16, 22, 48-49; Bar 2022 Q7

CARD poli-7
FRONT (shape): A candidate for public office challenges a proclamation or election result before the proper tribunal on grounds of fraud, irregularities, or disqualification.
FRONT (trigger words): election protest, COMELEC, electoral fraud, proclamation, electoral tribunal, disqualification
BACK (provision): Art. IX-C - Constitutional Commissions (COMELEC)
BACK (elements):
1. There is an election for a national or local office
2. A candidate challenges the result or qualification
3. For presidential/vice-presidential: the Presidential Electoral Tribunal (PET) has jurisdiction
4. For other national offices: the House of Representatives Electoral Tribunal (HRET) or Senate Electoral Tribunal (SET)
5. For local offices: COMELEC has jurisdiction in the first instance
6. The petition must be filed within the reglementary period
BACK (common confusion): COMELEC vs. Electoral Tribunals :: COMELEC is an administrative body that enforces election laws and decides local election contests. Electoral Tribunals (PET, SET, HRET) are judicial bodies for national offices composed of justices and legislators.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. IX-C; Omnibus Election Code

CARD poli-8
FRONT (shape): A person detained or arrested challenges the legality of the detention through a judicial proceeding.
FRONT (trigger words): writ of habeas corpus, illegal detention, unlawful arrest, judicial review of detention, liberty
BACK (provision): Art. III Sec. 13-15 - Rights of the Accused; Habeas Corpus
BACK (elements):
1. A person is deprived of liberty through detention or arrest
2. The detention is without legal authority or in violation of procedural rights
3. The writ of habeas corpus is filed by the detainee or someone on their behalf
4. The court orders the detaining authority to produce the detainee and justify the detention
5. The writ cannot be suspended except in cases of invasion or rebellion (Art. III Sec. 15)
BACK (common confusion): Art. 125 RPC (Delay in Delivery) :: Habeas corpus challenges the legal BASIS of detention. Art. 125 RPC punishes delays in filing charges. Even if the arrest is legal, unreasonable delay in filing charges may violate the right to due process.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. III Secs. 13-15

CARD poli-9
FRONT (shape): The national government shares revenues with local government units, and LGUs claim entitlement to a specific percentage of national taxes.
FRONT (trigger words): internal revenue allotment, IRA, LGU share, national taxes, revenue sharing, fiscal autonomy
BACK (provision): RA 7160 Secs. 284-287 - Internal Revenue Allotment (IRA)
BACK (elements):
1. National taxes are collected by the national government
2. A fixed percentage (40%) of national internal revenue taxes goes to LGUs as IRA (Art. X Sec. 6, Constitution)
3. The share is distributed: provinces (23%), cities (23%), municipalities (34%), barangays (20%)
4. LGUs have the power to create their own revenue sources (local taxes, fees, charges)
5. LGUs have fiscal autonomy subject to limitations in the LGC
BACK (common confusion): LGU Taxing Power vs. IRA :: IRA is a SHARE from national taxes — no LGU action needed. LGU taxing power (Secs. 129-155) allows LGUs to impose LOCAL taxes (real property tax, business tax, etc.) separately from the IRA. They are two distinct revenue sources.
SOURCE: RA 7160 Secs. 284-287; 1987 Constitution Art. X Sec. 6

CARD poli-10
FRONT (shape): A treaty or international agreement is entered into by the Philippines, and its effect on domestic law is questioned.
FRONT (trigger words): treaty, international agreement, Senate ratification, incorporation, dualist, customary international law
BACK (provision): Art. VII Sec. 21, Art. II Sec. 2 - Treaty-Making and International Law
BACK (elements):
1. A treaty or international agreement is negotiated by the executive branch
2. For validity: the treaty must be ratified by at least 2/3 of the Senate (Art. VII Sec. 21)
3. The treaty becomes part of domestic law upon concurrence by the Senate
4. The Philippines adopts the generally accepted principles of international law (Art. II Sec. 2)
5. A treaty cannot override the Constitution
BACK (common confusion): Treaty vs. Executive Agreement :: Treaties require Senate concurrence (2/3 vote). Executive agreements do not require Senate approval but cannot conflict with existing laws. Both are sources of international obligations but only treaties have the force of law domestically.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. VII Sec. 21, Art. II Sec. 2; Bar 2016 Q7

CARD poli-11
FRONT (shape): An LGU imposes a local tax or fee, and a business or resident challenges the imposition as beyond the LGU's taxing authority.
FRONT (trigger words): local tax, LGU taxation, business tax, real property tax, taxing power, revenue ordinance
BACK (provision): RA 7160 Secs. 129-155 - Local Government Taxing Powers
BACK (elements):
1. An LGU enacts a tax ordinance
2. The tax must be within the types of taxes each LGU may impose (Secs. 139-143)
3. The tax must be uniform, just, and not oppressive
4. The tax cannot duplicate taxes imposed by the national government
5. The tax ordinance takes effect only after approval by the Secretary of Justice for provinces/cities, or the sanggunian for municipalities
BACK (common confusion): Municipal vs. Provincial Taxing Power :: Provinces may tax (Sec. 139) specific items like quarry resources. Cities may impose taxes within their territorial jurisdiction at rates not exceeding provincial rates. Municipalities and barangays have more limited taxing powers.
SOURCE: RA 7160 Secs. 129-155

CARD poli-12
FRONT (shape): A citizen invokes the right to information on matters of public concern, and a government agency refuses disclosure.
FRONT (trigger words): right to information, public records, access to documents, transparency, FOI, public concern
BACK (provision): Art. III Sec. 7, Art. II Sec. 28 - Right to Information
BACK (elements):
1. A citizen requests information from a government office or agency
2. The information concerns a matter of public concern or interest
3. The information is not classified (national security, trade secrets, privileged communication)
4. The agency has a duty to provide access to official records and documents
5. The right includes access to government research and data used for policy decisions
BACK (common confusion): Art. III Sec. 7 vs. Art. II Sec. 28 :: Art. III Sec. 7 grants the right to information on matters of public concern — a constitutional right. Art. II Sec. 28 declares a state policy of full public disclosure — a policy principle. Art. III Sec. 7 is judicially enforceable; Art. II Sec. 28 guides policy but may not be directly actionable.
SOURCE: 1987 Constitution Art. III Sec. 7, Art. II Sec. 28
