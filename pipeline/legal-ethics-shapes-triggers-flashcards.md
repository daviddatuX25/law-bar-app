# Legal Ethics — Shapes, Trigger Words, Flashcards
Generated from CPRA (Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability, A.M. 22-09-01-SC)

---

CARD ethics-1
FRONT (shape): A lawyer accepts a case or continues representation despite a clear conflict of interest between two or more clients.
FRONT (trigger words): conflict of interest, dual representation, adverse parties, confidentiality conflict, disqualification
BACK (provision): Canon IV, Secs. 15-18 (CPRA) — Conflict of Interest
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer represents or is asked to represent two or more clients
2. The clients have conflicting or potentially conflicting interests
3. A conflict exists when: (a) representing one client will limit the lawyer's duty to another, (b) the lawyer's personal interest conflicts with a client's interest
4. For contemporaneous conflicts (Sec. 15): lawyer must not represent conflicting interests without INFORMED WRITTEN CONSENT from all clients
5. For successive conflicts (Sec. 16): lawyer must not represent a new client against a former client on the same or substantially related matter
6. Remedy: disqualification from representation
BACK (common confusion): Actual vs. Potential Conflict :: An actual conflict exists when the lawyer's duties to different clients clash DIRECTLY. A potential conflict exists when there is a RISK of a clash. The lawyer must decline representation for actual conflicts and may proceed with potential conflicts only after informed consent.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon IV, Secs. 15-18 (A.M. 22-09-01-SC)

CARD ethics-2
FRONT (shape): A lawyer receives money or property from a client in trust, and fails to account for or misappropriates the funds.
FRONT (trigger words): misappropriation, client funds, trust account, failure to account, conversion, disbarment
BACK (provision): Canon II, Secs. 27-30 (CPRA) — Fiduciary Duty to Client (Safekeeping Funds)
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer receives money, property, or documents from a client
2. The lawyer holds the funds in trust — as a fiduciary
3. The lawyer must: (a) deposit client funds in a separate trust account, (b) maintain proper records, (c) deliver funds promptly when due
4. The lawyer must NOT commingle personal funds with client funds
5. The lawyer must render an accounting within a reasonable time upon demand
6. Misappropriation or failure to account is a serious violation → disbarment or suspension
BACK (common confusion): Misappropriation vs. Delayed Accounting :: Misappropriation is using client funds for the lawyer's own benefit — ALWAYS a serious offense. Delayed accounting is failing to produce records on time — may be a lesser violation but still shows neglect. Intent to defraud is not required for misappropriation; mere failure to deliver is enough.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon II, Secs. 27-30

CARD ethics-3
FRONT (shape): A lawyer commits an act of deceit, falsehood, or fraud in dealing with a client, court, or third party.
FRONT (trigger words): deceit, fraud, dishonesty, falsehood, misrepresentation, moral turpitude, disbarment
BACK (provision): Canon III, Secs. 6-10 (CPRA) — Honesty and Integrity
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer engages in an act involving deceit, fraud, or dishonesty
2. The act is committed in a professional capacity or in personal dealings
3. Examples: (a) lying to the court, (b) falsifying evidence, (c) making false representations to clients, (d) cheating on bar exams, (e) false notarizations
4. The act reflects on the lawyer's fitness to practice law
5. Dishonesty and deceit are grounds for disbarment or suspension
6. The IBP investigates and the Supreme Court imposes the final penalty
BACK (common confusion): Light Offenses vs. Grave Dishonesty :: Not all dishonesty warrants disbarment. Simple negligence or minor misstatements may warrant only a reprimand or fine. But FRAUD, intentional deceit, and acts of MORAL TURPITUDE are grave offenses that almost always result in disbarment.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon III, Secs. 6-10

CARD ethics-4
FRONT (shape): A lawyer appears before a court without proper authority, or engages in conduct that undermines the dignity of the court.
FRONT (trigger words): contempt, disrespect, court decorum, unauthorized practice, lawyer as officer of court
BACK (provision): Canon V, Secs. 19-21 (CPRA) — Lawyer's Duty to the Court
BACK (elements):
1. The lawyer appears before a court or tribunal
2. The lawyer must: (a) maintain respect for the court, (b) not insult or harass the judge, (c) not resort to groundless ad hominem attacks
3. The lawyer must not: (a) appear without proper authority from the client, (b) engage in forum-shopping, (c) file frivolous pleadings
4. Contempt of court may be direct (committed in the presence of the court) or indirect (committed outside)
5. The lawyer must obey lawful orders and processes of the court
BACK (common confusion): Direct vs. Indirect Contempt :: Direct contempt is committed in the PRESENCE of the court (insults, disruptions) — punishable summarily. Indirect contempt is committed OUTSIDE the court (disobeying orders, abuse of court processes) — requires a hearing. Direct contempt of a lawyer is a graver offense.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon V, Secs. 19-21

CARD ethics-5
FRONT (shape): A lawyer enters into a business transaction with a client or acquires an interest adverse to the client.
FRONT (trigger words): lawyer-client transaction, business dealing with client, adverse interest, undue influence, full disclosure
BACK (provision): Canon II, Secs. 25-26 (CPRA) — Transactions with Clients
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer enters into a business transaction with a client
2. The transaction must be: (a) fair and reasonable, (b) fully disclosed to the client in writing, (c) with the client's informed written consent
3. The lawyer must advise the client to seek independent legal counsel
4. The lawyer must not acquire an interest adverse to the client
5. The lawyer must not purchase the client's property or interest in litigation (champerty is prohibited)
6. ANY transaction between lawyer and client is presumptively UNDUE INFLUENCE — the burden to prove fairness rests on the lawyer
BACK (common confusion): Business Transaction vs. Attorney's Fees :: Attorneys' fees are NOT prohibited business transactions — they are compensation for legal services. A prohibited transaction involves the lawyer buying the client's property, lending money to the client at high interest, or entering into a business partnership with the client.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon II, Secs. 25-26

CARD ethics-6
FRONT (shape): A lawyer is charged with a crime involving moral turpitude, and the IBP / Supreme Court considers disbarment or suspension.
FRONT (trigger words): disbarment, suspension, moral turpitude, IBP investigation, disbarment proceeding, administrative case
BACK (provision): Canon VI, Secs. 31-39, Rule 138 Secs. 18-31 (CPRA and ROC) — Disbarment and Discipline
BACK (elements):
1. A complaint is filed against a lawyer (or the Supreme Court acts motu proprio)
2. Grounds for discipline: (a) deceit or malpractice, (b) gross misconduct, (c) conviction for a crime involving moral turpitude, (d) violation of the CPRA or Lawyer's Oath, (e) grossly immoral conduct
3. The IBP conducts the investigation through its Commission on Bar Discipline
4. The IBP Board of Governors makes a recommendation to the Supreme Court
5. The Supreme Court has FINAL jurisdiction over disbarment and suspension — no appeal
6. Penalties: disbarment (permanent removal), suspension (temporary), reprimand, fine
BACK (common confusion): Disbarment vs. Suspension :: Disbarment is PERMANENT removal from the Roll of Attorneys — the lawyer can never practice again (except by extremely rare reinstatement). Suspension is TEMPORARY — the lawyer may return to practice after the suspension period ends. Both are imposed only by the Supreme Court.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon VI, Secs. 31-39

CARD ethics-7
FRONT (shape): A lawyer withdraws from a case without proper notice or abandons a client, causing prejudice to the client's case.
FRONT (trigger words): withdrawal, abandonment, neglect, client abandonment, notice to court, substitition of counsel
BACK (provision): Canon II, Secs. 22-24 (CPRA) — Withdrawal of Counsel
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer has accepted a case and entered an appearance
2. The lawyer may withdraw for just cause: (a) client insists on unlawful conduct, (b) client fails to pay fees (after proper demand), (c) client renders it unreasonably difficult to proceed
3. The lawyer must: (a) file a motion for withdrawal with the court, (b) give notice to the client, (c) return all papers and property of the client
4. Withdrawal without court approval (when appearance has been filed) → the lawyer remains counsel of record
5. Abandonment of a client without notice is a serious violation
BACK (common confusion): Withdrawal vs. Substitution :: Withdrawal is when the LAWYER initiates the termination of representation. Substitution is when the CLIENT hires a new lawyer to replace the existing one. Substitution requires the written consent of the client and the existing counsel, or a court order.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon II, Secs. 22-24

CARD ethics-8
FRONT (shape): A lawyer advertises legal services, solicits clients through marketing, or engages in ambulance chasing.
FRONT (trigger words): legal advertising, solicitation, ambulance chasing, barratry, lawyer marketing, professional advertisement
BACK (provision): Canon III, Secs. 12-14 (CPRA) — Advertising and Solicitation
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer publishes or disseminates information about legal services
2. The lawyer must NOT: (a) use false or misleading advertisement, (b) guarantee results, (c) solicit clients through runners or agents
3. Permissible advertisement: (a) law firm name and contact details, (b) areas of practice, (c) biographical information, (d) educational background
4. The advertisement must be: (a) truthful and not misleading, (b) not comparative, (c) not self-laudatory
5. Ambulance chasing (direct solicitation of accident victims, disaster survivors) is PROHIBITED
6. Barretry (exciting suits or quarrels) is also prohibited
BACK (common confusion): Professional Advertising vs. Ambulance Chasing :: Professional advertising is PASSIVE — it merely announces services. Ambulance chasing is ACTIVE solicitation of specific individuals who have suffered harm. The first is allowed (within limits); the second is always unethical and may be criminal.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon III, Secs. 12-14

CARD ethics-9
FRONT (shape): A lawyer testifies as a witness in a case they are handling, creating a dual role as counsel and witness.
FRONT (trigger words): lawyer as witness, dual role, conflict, advocate witness rule, testimony, disqualification
BACK (provision): Canon II, Sec. 28 (CPRA) — Lawyer as Witness
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer is handling a case as counsel
2. The lawyer also has knowledge of facts relevant to the case
3. Generally, a lawyer MUST NOT appear as both counsel and witness in the same case (advocate-witness rule)
4. EXCEPTIONS: (a) the testimony relates to an uncontested issue, (b) relates to the nature and value of legal services, (c) disqualification of the lawyer would work substantial hardship on the client
5. If the lawyer will be a necessary witness, they must withdraw as counsel
6. Opposing counsel may move for disqualification
BACK (common confusion): Material vs. Formal Witness :: The advocate-witness rule applies when the lawyer is a MATERIAL witness — testifies to facts essential to the client's case. If the lawyer's testimony is merely FORMAL (e.g., authenticating a document), the court may allow the dual role.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon II, Sec. 28

CARD ethics-10
FRONT (shape): A lawyer enters into an agreement for contingent fees that is unconscionable or excessive.
FRONT (trigger words): attorney's fees, contingent fees, quantum meruit, unconscionable fee, charging lien, fee agreement
BACK (provision): Canon II, Secs. 32-34 (CPRA) — Attorney's Fees
BACK (elements):
1. A lawyer and client enter into a fee agreement
2. The fee may be: (a) fixed fee, (b) hourly rate, (c) contingent fee (percentage of recovery)
3. For contingent fees: the agreement must be in WRITING and signed by the client
4. The fee must be REASONABLE — factors: (a) time and labor, (b) novelty and difficulty, (c) skill required, (d) customary charges, (e) amount involved, (f) results obtained
5. An UNCONSCIONABLE fee is one that shocks the conscience — excessive relative to the services rendered
6. If the lawyer is discharged without cause: entitled to recover on quantum meruit (as much as deserved)
BACK (common confusion): Contingent Fee vs. Champerty :: Contingent fees (fee as a percentage of the recovery) are PERMISSIBLE in civil cases. Champerty (lawyer paying the costs of litigation in exchange for a share of the recovery) used to be prohibited but is now generally allowed as a type of contingent fee arrangement.
SOURCE: CPRA Canon II, Secs. 32-34
