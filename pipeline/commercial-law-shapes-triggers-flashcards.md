# Commercial Law — Shapes, Trigger Words, Flashcards
Generated from RA 11232 (Revised Corporation Code), RA 10607 (Insurance Code), Act 2031 (NIL)

CARD comm-1
FRONT (shape): A group of persons incorporates a stock corporation, and the issue of corporate veil or separate juridical entity is raised.
FRONT (trigger words): corporation, separate juridical entity, incorporation, corporate veil, piercing the veil
BACK (provision): Secs. 2, 19-20 - Corporate Juridical Entity and Piercing the Corporate Veil
BACK (elements):
1. A corporation is registered with the SEC
2. The corporation has a separate juridical personality distinct from its stockholders
3. The corporate veil may be pierced when: (a) the corporation is used as an alter ego, (b) to commit fraud, (c) to evade existing obligations, (d) to defeat public convenience
4. The burden of proof to pierce the veil rests on the party seeking to pierce it
5. When pierced, stockholders become personally liable for corporate obligations
BACK (common confusion): De Facto vs. De Jure Corporation :: A de jure corporation substantially complied with all requirements. A de facto corporation failed in some technical requirement but is still treated as a corporation against third parties. A corporation by estoppel is not a corporation at all but persons acting as such are liable as partners.
SOURCE: RA 11232 Secs. 2, 19-20; Bar 2019 Q8

CARD comm-2
FRONT (shape): A stockholder exercises the right to inspect corporate books and records, and the corporation refuses access.
FRONT (trigger words): stockholder inspection, corporate books, right to inspect, stock and transfer book, minutes
BACK (provision): Secs. 49-50 - Stockholder's Right to Inspect Corporate Records
BACK (elements):
1. The person is a stockholder of record
2. The request is for inspection of corporate books and records
3. Records include: stock and transfer book, minutes of meetings, financial statements
4. The request must be made in writing and state a proper purpose
5. The corporation may refuse if the purpose is improper or the request is not made in good faith
6. If wrongfully refused, the stockholder may seek a court order (summary proceeding)
BACK (common confusion): Right to Inspect vs. Right to Information :: Corporate books include stock books, meeting minutes, and financial statements. The stockholder does NOT have an absolute right to inspect trade secrets, internal correspondence, or privileged documents. The purpose must be related to their interest as a stockholder.
SOURCE: RA 11232 Secs. 49-50; Bar 2021 Q11

CARD comm-3
FRONT (shape): A corporation declares dividends, and a stockholder claims entitlement but the corporation argues there are no unrestricted retained earnings.
FRONT (trigger words): dividends, retained earnings, declaration, stockholder, accumulated profits, unrestricted
BACK (provision): Sec. 39 - Declaration and Payment of Dividends
BACK (elements):
1. The corporation has unrestricted retained earnings
2. The board of directors declares a dividend by board resolution
3. The dividend may be: cash, property, or stock dividends
4. Stockholders of record on the record date are entitled to receive the dividend
5. Dividends cannot be declared if the corporation is insolvent or if it would impair capital
6. Preferred stockholders have priority over common stockholders if so provided in the articles
BACK (common confusion): Cash Dividends vs. Stock Dividends :: Cash dividends reduce retained earnings and distribute cash. Stock dividends distribute additional shares and capitalize retained earnings — they do not reduce corporate assets. Stock dividends require an amendment to the articles if they increase authorized capital.
SOURCE: RA 11232 Sec. 39; Bar 2018 Q9

CARD comm-4
FRONT (shape): Officers or directors of a corporation enter into a transaction with the corporation where they have a personal interest, and a stockholder questions its validity.
FRONT (trigger words): conflict of interest, self-dealing, director liability, fiduciary duty, disloyalty
BACK (provision): Secs. 30-32 - Directors' Fiduciary Duties and Self-Dealing
BACK (elements):
1. A director or officer has a personal interest in a contract or transaction with the corporation
2. The interested director must disclose the interest to the board
3. The contract is valid if: (a) the board approves it in good faith despite the conflict, (b) the interested director does not vote on the approval
4. The contract must be fair and reasonable to the corporation at the time of approval
5. Violation results in liability for actual damages and disgorgement of profits
BACK (common confusion): Sec. 30 vs. Sec. 31 :: Sec. 30 covers contracts with interlocking directors (directors who sit on both boards). Sec. 31 covers contracts where a director has a direct or indirect interest. Both require disclosure and good faith approval.
SOURCE: RA 11232 Secs. 30-32

CARD comm-5
FRONT (shape): An insurance policy is issued, and the insurer denies coverage claiming the insured made a material misrepresentation in the application.
FRONT (trigger words): insurance, misrepresentation, concealment, material fact, voidable policy, warranty
BACK (provision): RA 10607 Secs. 26-30 - Concealment and Misrepresentation in Insurance
BACK (elements):
1. An insurance policy is issued based on an application
2. The insured failed to disclose a material fact (concealment) or made a false statement (misrepresentation)
3. The fact concealed or misrepresented is material — would have affected the insurer's decision to accept the risk
4. The insurer relied on the concealment or misrepresentation
5. The policy becomes voidable at the option of the insurer
6. If the misrepresentation is intentional (fraudulent), the insurer may rescind and keep the premiums
BACK (common confusion): Concealment vs. Misrepresentation :: Concealment is the NONDISCLOSURE of a material fact that the insured knew or should have known. Misrepresentation is an AFFIRMATIVE false statement. Both make the policy voidable, but concealment requires that the insured intentionally withheld the information.
SOURCE: RA 10607 Secs. 26-30; Bar 2020 Q5

CARD comm-6
FRONT (shape): An insured suffers a loss, and the insurer refuses to pay claiming the loss is excluded under the policy or the insured has no insurable interest.
FRONT (trigger words): insurable interest, loss, claim denial, coverage dispute, risk, indemnity
BACK (provision): RA 10607 Secs. 10-17 - Insurable Interest and Risk
BACK (elements):
1. There is a valid insurance contract
2. The insured has an insurable interest at the time the policy takes effect
3. For life insurance: insurable interest exists for self, spouse, dependents, creditors (limited to debt), key employees
4. For property insurance: insurable interest exists for the owner, mortgagee, lessee, or any person with an interest in the property
5. The loss must be covered by the policy and occur during the policy period
6. The insurer is liable only up to the actual loss suffered (principle of indemnity)
BACK (common confusion): Life vs. Property Insurable Interest :: For life insurance, insurable interest need only EXIST at the inception of the policy — it can be lost later without affecting the policy. For property insurance, insurable interest must EXIST at the time of loss — if the owner sells the property, the insurance policy does not transfer automatically.
SOURCE: RA 10607 Secs. 10-17

CARD comm-7
FRONT (shape): A person signs a promissory note or check, and the holder demands payment but the maker claims the instrument is invalid or there is a defense.
FRONT (trigger words): negotiable instrument, promissory note, check, holder in due course, defense, indorsement
BACK (provision): Act 2031 Secs. 1, 24, 52, 57-59 - Negotiable Instruments Law — HIDC
BACK (elements):
1. The instrument is negotiable: (a) in writing and signed, (b) unconditional promise to pay, (c) a sum certain, (d) payable on demand or at a definite time, (e) payable to order or bearer
2. The holder is a holder in due course (HIDC): took the instrument complete and regular, before maturity, in good faith, for value, without notice of defect
3. An HIDC takes the instrument free from personal defenses (but not real defenses)
4. Real defenses include: incapacity, duress, illegality, fraud in inception, discharge in bankruptcy
5. Personal defenses include: failure of consideration, fraud in inducement, payment, set-off
BACK (common confusion): Holder in Due Course vs. Ordinary Holder :: An HIDC (Sec. 52) takes the instrument free from personal defenses between prior parties — can enforce even if the original transaction was defective. An ordinary holder is subject to all defenses that could be raised against the original payee.
SOURCE: Act 2031 Secs. 1, 24, 52, 57-59; Bar 2019 Q10

CARD comm-8
FRONT (shape): A check is presented for payment but is dishonored for insufficient funds, and criminal liability for bouncing checks is sought.
FRONT (trigger words): bouncing check, dishonored, insufficient funds, BP 22, bad check, insufficiency
BACK (provision): BP 22 (Bouncing Checks Law) — Sec. 1
BACK (elements):
1. A person makes or draws a check
2. The check is presented for payment within 90 days from date
3. The check is dishonored for insufficient funds or credit
4. The drawer receives notice of dishonor and fails to pay within 5 days
5. Prima facie evidence: knowledge of insufficiency if the drawer had no sufficient funds at the time of issuance or within 90 days
6. Penalty: imprisonment or fine, or both, at court's discretion
BACK (common confusion): BP 22 vs. Estafa (Art. 315 RPC) :: BP 22 punishes the mere ACT of issuing a bad check — intent to defraud is presumed. Estafa under Art. 315 requires actual damage and proof of deceit. One single check issuance may violate both laws, but the accused cannot be convicted of both for the same act (double jeopardy).
SOURCE: BP 22; Bar 2021 Q8

CARD comm-9
FRONT (shape): A corporation undergoes dissolution, and creditors claim payment from remaining assets or stockholders.
FRONT (trigger words): dissolution, liquidation, winding up, creditors, distribution of assets, corporate termination
BACK (provision): Secs. 117-123 - Corporate Dissolution and Liquidation
BACK (elements):
1. The corporation resolves to dissolve voluntarily or is dissolved involuntarily by the SEC
2. For voluntary dissolution: board resolution + stockholder vote of 2/3 of outstanding capital
3. If creditors exist, the corporation must publish notice and settle claims
4. Remaining assets after paying creditors are distributed to stockholders
5. The SEC issues a certificate of dissolution
6. The corporation continues as a body corporate for 3 years after dissolution for liquidation purposes only
BACK (common confusion): De Facto Dissolution vs. De Jure Dissolution :: De jure dissolution follows legal procedures (SEC approval, publication). De facto dissolution occurs when the corporation ceases operations but has not formally dissolved — it is still subject to corporate duties and liabilities.
SOURCE: RA 11232 Secs. 117-123

CARD comm-10
FRONT (shape): A person indorses a negotiable instrument to another, and questions arise about the indorser's liability.
FRONT (trigger words): indorsement, indorser liability, qualified indorsement, restrictive indorsement, without recourse
BACK (provision): Act 2031 Secs. 29-38, 65-68 - Indorsements and Liability
BACK (elements):
1. The holder indorses a negotiable instrument by signing it
2. Types of indorsement: (a) blank (signature only — becomes bearer paper), (b) special (specifies the indorsee), (c) restrictive (limits further transfer), (d) qualified ("without recourse" — limits indorser liability)
3. An unqualified indorser warrants: (a) the instrument is genuine, (b) the indorser has good title, (c) prior parties had capacity, (d) the instrument is valid, (e) the indorser will pay if dishonored with notice
4. A qualified indorser ("without recourse") disclaims contract liability but not the implied warranties
5. Accommodation indorser: signs without receiving value — liable as an original indorser
BACK (common confusion): Unqualified vs. Qualified Indorsement :: An unqualified indorser (blank or special) is LIABLE to pay if the maker defaults and proper notice is given. A qualified indorser ("without recourse") DISCLAIMS this liability. But the qualified indorser still gives IMPLIED WARRANTIES (negotiability, no defenses).
SOURCE: Act 2031 Secs. 29-38, 65-68

CARD comm-11
FRONT (shape): An insurance contract is contested on the ground that the insured did not have an insurable interest at the time of the loss, or the contract violates public policy.
FRONT (trigger words): insurance contract, insurable interest requirement, wagering policy, public policy, conditional
BACK (provision): RA 10607 Secs. 18-22 - Effect of Lack of Insurable Interest and Wagering Contracts
BACK (elements):
1. For property insurance: insurable interest must exist at the time of loss (Sec. 21)
2. For life insurance: insurable interest must exist at the inception of the policy (Sec. 19)
3. A policy without insurable interest is a wagering contract — void
4. Assignment of a life insurance policy without insurable interest is void if done to circumvent the requirement
5. Mere expectation of loss or profit is not insurable interest
BACK (common confusion): Assignment vs. Beneficiary Change :: Assignment of an insurance policy transfers rights to a new owner who must have insurable interest. Changing the beneficiary does not require insurable interest — the beneficiary need only be named by the policy owner.
SOURCE: RA 10607 Secs. 18-22

CARD comm-12
FRONT (shape): A person subscribes to shares of stock in a corporation, fails to pay, and the corporation seeks to collect or forfeit the subscription.
FRONT (trigger words): subscription, unpaid subscription, stockholder liability, corporation, pre-incorporation subscription, forfeiture
BACK (provision): Secs. 46-48, 59-61 - Subscription for Shares and Liability
BACK (elements):
1. A person subscribes to shares before or after incorporation
2. Pre-incorporation subscription: must be accepted by the corporation upon incorporation
3. The subscriber is liable to pay the subscription price
4. If unpaid: the corporation may collect the amount due or forfeit the shares
5. Forfeiture requires: (a) notice, (b) delinquency declared, (c) public auction of the shares
6. The subscriber remains liable for the deficiency if the auction proceeds are insufficient (Sec. 61)
BACK (common confusion): Subscription vs. Paid-up Capital :: Subscription is a PROMISE to pay. Paid-up capital is the portion ALREADY PAID. A subscriber who has not fully paid is still a stockholder but may have limited rights (e.g. cannot vote if subscription is delinquent).
SOURCE: RA 11232 Secs. 46-48, 59-61
