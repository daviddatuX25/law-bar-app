# Labor Law — Shapes, Trigger Words, Flashcards
Generated from PD 442 (Labor Code of the Philippines)

---

CARD labor-1
FRONT (shape): An employee is dismissed from employment, and the employee claims the dismissal was without just or authorized cause or without procedural due process.
FRONT (trigger words): illegal dismissal, termination, just cause, authorized cause, due process, separation pay
BACK (provision): Arts. 292-299 (PD 442) - Termination of Employment
BACK (elements):
1. There is an employer-employee relationship
2. The employee is dismissed or separated from employment
3. For JUST causes (Art. 296): serious misconduct, willful disobedience, gross neglect, fraud, commission of crime
4. For AUTHORIZED causes (Art. 298-299): installation of labor-saving devices, redundancy, retrenchment, closure, disease
5. For just causes: the employee must be given 2-notice rule (notice of charge + notice of decision)
6. For authorized causes: the employer must give 30-day written notice to DOLE and employee + separation pay
BACK (common confusion): Just Cause vs. Authorized Cause :: Just cause (Art. 296) is attributable to the EMPLOYEE's fault — no separation pay required if valid. Authorized cause (Arts. 298-299) is NOT the employee's fault — separation pay is mandatory. Both require procedural due process.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 292-299; Bar 2018 Q11

CARD labor-2
FRONT (shape): A group of workers organizes a union to collectively bargain with the employer, and the employer interferes with or opposes the unionization.
FRONT (trigger words): union, collective bargaining, union registration, unfair labor practice, ULP, CBA
BACK (provision): Arts. 257-260, 267-268, 277 - Union Organization and Unfair Labor Practices
BACK (elements):
1. Workers organize or form a labor organization
2. The union is registered with the DOLE-Bureau of Labor Relations (BLR)
3. The employer commits unfair labor practice (ULP) under Arts. 267-268 by: (a) interfering with union formation, (b) discriminating against union members, (c) refusing to bargain collectively
4. For employees: ULP includes (a) demanding fees for union representation, (b) causing discrimination, (c) refusing to bargain
5. The appropriate bargaining unit must be determined for CBA negotiations
BACK (common confusion): Legitimate Labor Organization vs. Company Union :: A legitimate labor organization is registered with DOLE and independent of the employer. A company union (Art. 268 par. a) is formed, supported, or controlled by the employer — it is an unfair labor practice.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 257-268; Bar 2020 Q9

CARD labor-3
FRONT (shape): A worker claims entitlement to overtime pay, holiday pay, or 13th-month pay, and the employer disputes the claim.
FRONT (trigger words): wages, overtime, holiday pay, 13th month, minimum wage, wage distortion, money claims
BACK (provision): Arts. 86-97, 103-109 (PD 442) - Wages and Benefits
BACK (elements):
1. There is an employer-employee relationship
2. The employee performs work beyond 8 hours a day → entitled to overtime pay (25% more for ordinary days, 30% more for holidays)
3. The employee works on a rest day or holiday → premium pay
4. The employee is entitled to 13th-month pay (PD 851) — minimum of 1/12 of basic salary
5. The employee is entitled to service incentive leave of 5 days/year after 1 year of service
6. Money claims must be filed within 3 years from the time the cause of action accrued
BACK (common confusion): Overtime vs. Compressed Work Week :: In a compressed work week arrangement (by agreement or company policy), the daily hours may exceed 8 but total weekly hours remain the same. The employee is not entitled to overtime premium for hours beyond 8 in a compressed schedule if the total weekly hours are within the normal 48-hour threshold.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 86-97; Bar 2019 Q7

CARD labor-4
FRONT (shape): A worker is injured or contracts an illness in the course of employment and claims compensation from the employer.
FRONT (trigger words): work injury, compensation, occupational disease, employer liability, disability, Sickness
BACK (provision): Arts. 165-199 (PD 442) - Employees' Compensation and State Insurance Fund
BACK (elements):
1. The employee suffers an injury or illness
2. The injury or illness arose out of and in the course of employment
3. For occupational diseases: the disease is listed under Annex A of the Rules or proven to be work-related
4. The employer or the State Insurance Fund (SSS/GSIS) is liable for compensation
5. The employee must file the claim within the prescriptive period (3 years from discovery)
6. Compensation includes: medical services, disability income, death benefits, funeral expenses
BACK (common confusion): Work-Related vs. Non-Work-Related :: The injury/illness must be caused directly by the employment or aggravated by working conditions. Ordinary diseases of life (e.g. common cold) not caused by work are not compensable. For occupational diseases, the employee must prove a reasonable causal connection to the work.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 165-199; Bar 2022 Q8

CARD labor-5
FRONT (shape): An employee voluntarily resigns or retires, and claims entitlement to retirement pay under the Labor Code or company policy.
FRONT (trigger words): retirement, resignation, separation, retirement pay, retirement age, quitclaim
BACK (provision): Art. 286-287 (PD 442), RA 7641 - Retirement and Resignation
BACK (elements):
1. The employee separates from employment voluntarily
2. For retirement: the employee must have reached the retirement age (60 if provided in CBA/company policy, 65 by law under RA 7641)
3. Retirement pay: at least 1/2 month salary for every year of service (6 days of salary per year)
4. A retiring employee with at least 5 years of service is entitled to retirement pay under RA 7641
5. A resigning employee is generally not entitled to separation pay unless required by company policy or CBA
6. Quitclaims are valid if voluntarily executed with full understanding and for reasonable consideration
BACK (common confusion): Retirement vs. Resignation :: Retirement is separation due to AGE (reaching the retirement age). Resignation is voluntary separation for personal reasons at any age. Retirement pay is MANDATORY under RA 7641. Resignation generally does not entitle the employee to separation pay.
SOURCE: PD 442 Art. 286-287; RA 7641; Bar 2021 Q6

CARD labor-6
FRONT (shape): A worker is engaged through a contractor or agency, and the issue of whether the worker is an employee of the contractor or the principal employer arises.
FRONT (trigger words): labor contracting, contractor, principal, regular employment, trilateral relationship, DOLE
BACK (provision): Arts. 106-109 (PD 442) - Contractor Liability and Labor-Only Contracting
BACK (elements):
1. There is a principal who hires a contractor to perform work
2. The contractor supplies workers for the job
3. If the contractor carries on an independent business and has substantial capital → legitimate contracting
4. If the contractor does not have substantial capital and the workers perform activities directly related to the principal's business → labor-only contracting (prohibited)
5. In labor-only contracting: the PRINCIPAL is considered the employer of the workers (Art. 107)
6. The principal is solidarily liable with the contractor for all labor law violations
BACK (common confusion): Job Contracting vs. Labor-Only Contracting :: Job contracting is legitimate when the contractor has independent business, substantial capital, and exercises control over workers. Labor-only contracting is when the contractor is a mere intermediary — the principal is the true employer. The control test determines the relationship.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 106-109; Bar 2017 Q15

CARD labor-7
FRONT (shape): An employee is transferred, demoted, or undergoes a change in working conditions and claims constructive dismissal.
FRONT (trigger words): constructive dismissal, demotion, transfer, forced resignation, work conditions, demotion without cause
BACK (provision): Art. 280, 282 (PD 442) - Constructive Dismissal and Regular Employment
BACK (elements):
1. There is an employer-employee relationship
2. The employer imposes a change in working conditions that is unreasonable and prejudicial
3. The change effectively forces the employee to resign (no choice but to leave)
4. Examples: demotion without valid cause, harassment, pay reduction, transfer to a demeaning position
5. The employee may still claim illegal dismissal even if they appeared to resign
6. The timing of resignation and the circumstances surrounding it are critical evidence
BACK (common confusion): Actual Dismissal vs. Constructive Dismissal :: In actual dismissal, the employer tells the employee to leave. In constructive dismissal, the employee is forced to "resign" because conditions became intolerable. For constructive dismissal, the burden is on the employee to prove the conditions or acts amounting to constructive dismissal.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 280, 282; Bar 2018 Q14

CARD labor-8
FRONT (shape): A dispute between management and labor over CBA interpretation or terms of employment is submitted to compulsory arbitration or the NLRC.
FRONT (trigger words): NLRC, labor dispute, arbitration, compulsory arbitration, jurisdiction, appeal
BACK (provision): Arts. 223-236 (PD 442) - NLRC Jurisdiction and Labor Dispute Resolution
BACK (elements):
1. A labor dispute exists between employer and employees
2. The National Labor Relations Commission (NLRC) has original and exclusive jurisdiction over unfair labor practices and termination disputes
3. The NLRC also has appellate jurisdiction over decisions of Labor Arbiters
4. The Labor Arbiter has jurisdiction over: (a) termination cases, (b) money claims exceeding P5,000, (c) ULP cases
5. The NLRC decision may be appealed to the Court of Appeals via certiorari (Rule 65)
6. The Secretary of Labor may assume jurisdiction over strikes/lockouts affecting national interest (Art. 278)
BACK (common confusion): Labor Arbiter vs. NLRC vs. DOLE Secretary :: Labor Arbiter decides at FIRST instance (termination, money claims). NLRC handles APPEALS from Labor Arbiter decisions. DOLE Secretary has jurisdiction over STRIKES/LOCKOUTS that affect national interest. Grievance machinery via CBA handles contractual disputes.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 223-236

CARD labor-9
FRONT (shape): A worker goes on strike or the employer declares a lockout, and the legality of the work stoppage is questioned.
FRONT (trigger words): strike, lockout, picket, notice of strike, assumption of jurisdiction, return to work
BACK (provision): Arts. 262-278 (PD 442) - Strikes and Lockouts
BACK (elements):
1. A labor dispute exists
2. For a valid strike: (a) notice of strike filed with NCMB-DOLE at least 30 days before, (b) strike vote by majority of union members, (c) 7-day notice before actual strike
3. For a valid lockout: (a) notice of lockout filed with NCMB, (b) lockout vote, (c) 7-day notice
4. A strike is illegal if: (a) no valid notice, (b) no strike vote, (c) involves non-labor issues, (d) violates a compulsory arbitration order
5. The President or DOLE Secretary may assume jurisdiction or decide the dispute if it affects national interest
6. Upon assumption of jurisdiction, striking workers must return to work within 24 hours, and the employer must accept them
BACK (common confusion): Legal vs. Illegal Strike :: A legal strike follows all procedural requirements (notice, strike vote, waiting period). An illegal strike is one that violates procedural or substantive requirements. Participants in an illegal strike may lose their employment status, while legal strike participants are entitled to reinstatement.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 262-278

CARD labor-10
FRONT (shape): An employee is employed on a fixed-term or project-based contract, and after the term/project ends claims regular employment status.
FRONT (trigger words): regular employment, probationary, fixed-term, project employee, casual employee, regularization
BACK (provision): Arts. 280-286 (PD 442) - Regular and Casual Employment
BACK (elements):
1. An employee performs work for an employer
2. If the work is necessary or desirable to the employer's business → regular employee (Art. 280)
3. If the employee has rendered at least 1 year of service → regular employee
4. Probationary employment: maximum of 6 months, must be informed of standards, becomes regular after 6 months if allowed to continue
5. Project employment: co-terminous with the specific project (must be clearly defined)
6. Seasonal employment: co-terminous with the season
BACK (common confusion): Project vs. Regular Employment :: Project employment is for a SPECIFIC undertaking with a defined completion date. Even if the project lasts years, the employee is not regular. The key is whether the employment is coterminous with the project and whether there was a clear project designation at hiring. Without clear project designation, the employee is regular.
SOURCE: PD 442 Arts. 280-286; Bar 2020 Q12
