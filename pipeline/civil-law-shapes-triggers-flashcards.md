# Civil Law — Shapes, Trigger Words, Flashcards
Generated from Civil Code of the Philippines (RA 386)

---

CARD civil-1
FRONT (shape): Two buyers, one immovable, one registered first in good faith.
FRONT (trigger words): double sale, registered first, immovable, good faith, priority registration
BACK (provision): Art. 1544 - Double Sale of Immovable
BACK (elements):
1. Two or more valid sales contracts
2. Entered by the same vendor
3. Concurring on the same subject matter (an immovable property)
4. Competitors hold conflicting interests
5. The winning buyer recorded the sale first in the Registry of Property in good faith
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1409 (Void Contracts) :: If one of the competing sales is void from inception (e.g. forged deed from non-owner), it is nullity under Art. 1409, not double sale.
SOURCE: Civil Code Art. 1544; Bar 2018 Q4

CARD civil-2
FRONT (shape): Person causes damage to another through a negligent act or omission, no pre-existing contractual relation.
FRONT (trigger words): negligence, quasi-delict, culpa aquiliana, damage, no contract
BACK (provision): Art. 2176 - Quasi-Delicts (Culpa Aquiliana)
BACK (elements):
1. Act or omission by defendant
2. Negligence or fault attributable to defendant
3. Damage or injury suffered by plaintiff
4. Causal connection between negligence and damage
5. No pre-existing contractual relation between parties
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1170 (Breach of Contract) :: In quasi-delict there is no pre-existing contract; Art. 1170 applies when the negligent act breaches an existing contractual obligation.
SOURCE: Civil Code Art. 2176; Bar 2016 Q3, Bar 2020 Q7

CARD civil-3
FRONT (shape): Employer is sued for damages caused by an employee acting within the scope of employment.
FRONT (trigger words): employer liability, employee, scope of employment, pater familias, supervision
BACK (provision): Art. 2180 - Liability of Employers for Employees
BACK (elements):
1. Employer-employee relationship exists
2. Employee acting within the scope of assigned tasks
3. Negligence or fault of employee causes damage
4. Employer failed to exercise due diligence in supervision and selection
BACK (common confusion): Art. 2176 (Quasi-Delict) :: Art. 2176 is the general rule; Art. 2180 creates vicarious liability — the employer is directly liable even without personal fault.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 2176, 2180; Bar 2021 Q5

CARD civil-4
FRONT (shape): A person abuses a right, causing damage to another, where the act is otherwise legal in form but intended to harm.
FRONT (trigger words): abuse of rights, malice, intent to harm, anti-social purpose, human relations
BACK (provision): Art. 19-21 - Human Relations (Abuse of Rights Principle)
BACK (elements):
1. An act is legal in form
2. Act is done in bad faith or with intent to injure
3. The act results in damage to another
4. The act is contrary to morals, good customs, or public policy
BACK (common confusion): Art. 2176 (Quasi-Delict) :: Abuse of rights does not require negligence; it requires deliberate intent to harm. Art. 2176 is based on negligence, not on intentional harm through an otherwise lawful right.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 19-21; Bar 2019 Q2

CARD civil-5
FRONT (shape): A contract exists, one party fails to perform, causing damage to the other party.
FRONT (trigger words): breach of contract, non-performance, damages, obligation, debtor
BACK (provision): Art. 1170 - Liability for Breach of Contract
BACK (elements):
1. There is a valid and enforceable obligation
2. The obligor fails to perform, delays performance, or performs defectively
3. The obligor is guilty of fraud, negligence, delay, or contravention of the terms
4. Damage results from the breach
BACK (common confusion): Art. 2176 (Quasi-Delict) :: Breach of contract requires a pre-existing contractual relation (privity). Quasi-delict does not. The same negligent act cannot give rise to both unless there is also a separate tort.
SOURCE: Civil Code Art. 1170; Bar 2017 Q8

CARD civil-6
FRONT (shape): A contract is completely without effect because one of the essential requisites is absent.
FRONT (trigger words): void contract, inexistent, no consent, illegal object, absolute nullity
BACK (provision): Art. 1409 - Void or Inexistent Contracts
BACK (elements):
1. Absence of consent, object, or cause
2. Illegality of object, cause, or purpose
3. Simulated contract without true intent
4. Contract expressly prohibited or declared void by law
5. The defect is incurable — cannot be ratified
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1390 (Voidable Contracts) :: Void contracts are incurable and produce no effect at all. Voidable contracts are valid until annulled and can be ratified.
SOURCE: Civil Code Art. 1409

CARD civil-7
FRONT (shape): Seller delivers defective goods or property, buyer discovers defect after delivery and seeks remedy.
FRONT (trigger words): hidden defect, warranty against hidden defects, redhibitory, eviction, buyer
BACK (provision): Art. 1561-1566 - Warranty Against Hidden Defects
BACK (elements):
1. Sale of goods or property
2. Hidden defect exists at the time of sale
3. Defect is substantial — renders property unfit or diminishes its value
4. Buyer did not know of the defect
5. Buyer gives notice within a reasonable time after discovery
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1547 (Warranty Against Eviction) :: Warranty against hidden defects is about physical defects in the thing sold. Warranty against eviction is about legal defects in the seller's title.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 1561-1566

CARD civil-8
FRONT (shape): A person suffers injury or death due to a defective product or unsafe premises, and sues for damages.
FRONT (trigger words): damages, moral damages, exemplary damages, temperate damages, nominal damages
BACK (provision): Arts. 2199-2235 - Kinds of Damages
BACK (elements):
1. Actual or compensatory damages — pecuniary loss proved (Art. 2199)
2. Moral damages — physical suffering, mental anguish, serious anxiety (Art. 2217)
3. Nominal damages — right violated but no substantial loss (Art. 2221)
4. Temperate or moderate damages — pecuniary loss cannot be proved exactly (Art. 2224)
5. Liquidated damages — agreed upon by parties (Art. 2226)
6. Exemplary or corrective damages — set public example, need bad faith (Art. 2229)
BACK (common confusion): Moral vs. Exemplary :: Moral damages compensate for mental suffering. Exemplary damages punish the defendant and require bad faith or wanton conduct. One can have moral damages without exemplary, but exemplary requires at least one other award.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 2199-2235; Bar 2020 Q10

CARD civil-9
FRONT (shape): A donor gives a donation that is later revoked because the donee committed ingratitude or failed to comply with a condition.
FRONT (trigger words): donation, revocation, ingratitude, non-compliance, burden
BACK (provision): Art. 760-773 - Revocation of Donations
BACK (elements):
1. Valid donation was made
2. Donee commits an act of ingratitude (e.g. imputes crime, refuses support) 
3. Or donee fails to comply with conditions/burdens imposed
4. Donor files action within the prescriptive period (4 years for ingratitude, 5 for non-compliance)
BACK (common confusion): Art. 739 (Void Donations) :: Void donations under Art. 739 (e.g. to priests, concubines) are invalid from the start. Revocation under 760-773 applies to initially valid donations.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 760-773

CARD civil-10
FRONT (shape): Co-owners of property or co-heirs cannot be forced to remain in the co-ownership, and any agreement to stay indissolubly bound is void.
FRONT (trigger words): co-ownership, partition, no perpetual co-ownership, action to partition
BACK (provision): Art. 494 - Right to Demand Partition
BACK (elements):
1. Two or more persons co-own property
2. No agreement to keep undivided (limited to 10 years max)
3. Any co-owner may demand partition at any time
4. Partition may be made by actual division or sale and proceeds distribution
BACK (common confusion): Art. 498 (Insufficient Share for Partition) :: If the property cannot be physically divided (share too small), the co-owner may demand sale instead of actual partition. The right to partition itself is never extinguished.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 494, 498

CARD civil-11
FRONT (shape): A person renders services or delivers goods without a contract, expecting payment, and seeks compensation.
FRONT (trigger words): unjust enrichment, solution indebiti, no contract, payment by mistake, quasi-contract
BACK (provision): Art. 2142-2156 - Quasi-Contracts (Solutio Indebiti & Negotiorum Gestio)
BACK (elements):
1. Solutio Indebiti (Art. 2154): Payment was made by mistake when no obligation existed; recipient must return
2. Negotiorum Gestio (Art. 2144): One voluntarily manages another's business without authority; reimbursement for necessary expenses

BACK (common confusion): Quasi-Contract vs. Quasi-Delict :: Quasi-contracts are based on lawful, voluntary conduct that creates an obligation by law. Quasi-delict is based on negligent or wrongful conduct causing damage.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 2142-2156

CARD civil-12
FRONT (shape): A sale is made on installment, buyer fails to pay, seller seeks to recover payments or the property.
FRONT (trigger words): installment sale, rescission, non-payment, waiver of right to recover, Maceda Law
BACK (provision): RA 6552 - Maceda Law (Realty Installment Buyer Protection)
BACK (elements):
1. Sale of real estate on installments
2. Buyer fails to pay installments
3. If buyer paid less than 2 years: seller may rescind with 60 days notice
4. If buyer paid 2+ years: buyer entitled to grace period (1 month per year) and refund of at least 50% of payments
5. Contractual waiver of these rights is void
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1191 (Resolution of Reciprocal Obligations) :: Under Maceda Law, the buyer gets statutory protections (grace period, refund) that override general contract law. Art. 1191 applies only when Maceda Law does not.
SOURCE: RA 6552; Civil Code Art. 1191

CARD civil-13
FRONT (shape): Parents are sued for damages caused by their minor child living with them.
FRONT (trigger words): parental liability, minor child, supervision, negligence of parents
BACK (provision): Art. 2180 par. 2 - Liability of Parents
BACK (elements):
1. Parent-child relationship exists
2. The child is a minor living with the parents
3. The minor causes damage through an act or omission
4. The parents failed in their duty of supervision
BACK (common confusion): Art. 2180 par. 4 (Teachers) :: Teachers are subsidiarily liable for acts of students while under their supervision (school hours). Parents are primarily liable for acts of children at all other times.
SOURCE: Civil Code Art. 2180

CARD civil-14
FRONT (shape): Two parties have a contract, one fails to perform a reciprocal obligation, the other chooses to either demand performance or rescind.
FRONT (trigger words): reciprocal obligation, rescission, specific performance, choice of remedy, breach
BACK (provision): Art. 1191 - Resolution of Reciprocal Obligations
BACK (elements):
1. Reciprocal obligation exists (each party bound to give/preform for the other)
2. One party fails to comply
3. The injured party may choose between: (a) specific performance with damages, or (b) rescission with damages
4. The choice is not absolute — court may grant the remedy only if equitable
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1380 (Rescission of Contracts) :: Art. 1191 is about reciprocal obligations and breach (resolution). Art. 1380 is about rescission due to damage/lesion from third parties — entirely different grounds.
SOURCE: Civil Code Art. 1191

CARD civil-15
FRONT (shape): A person enters a contract with a mental incapacity or under duress, undue influence, or fraud, and seeks to annul it.
FRONT (trigger words): voidable contract, annulment, incapacity, vitiated consent, intimidation, fraud
BACK (provision): Art. 1390 - Voidable Contracts
BACK (elements):
1. Contract valid on its face
2. Consent is vitiated by: (a) minority/incapacity, (b) mistake, (c) violence/intimidation, (d) undue influence, or (e) fraud
3. Action for annulment must be filed within 4 years
4. Contract can be ratified expressly or tacitly
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1409 (Void Contracts) :: Voidable contracts are valid until annulled and can be ratified. Void contracts are inexistent from the start and cannot be ratified.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 1390, 1391, 1392

CARD civil-16
FRONT (shape): A spouse is utterly unable to perform essential marital obligations due to a grave psychological condition existing at marriage celebration.
FRONT (trigger words): psychological incapacity, marital obligations, grave, juridical antecedence, incurable
BACK (provision): Art. 36, Family Code - Psychological Incapacity
BACK (elements):
1. Grave (grave psychological anomaly preventing performance of essential marital obligations)
2. Juridical antecedence (exists at the time of the celebration of marriage)
3. Incurable (clinically or psychologically permanent)
BACK (common confusion): Art. 45 par. 5 (Physical Incapacity) :: Psychological incapacity refers to the inability to comply with essential marital obligations (love, respect, support) due to psychological reasons; physical incapacity is the physical inability to consummate the marriage (impotence).
SOURCE: Family Code Art. 36; Tan-Cruz v. Cruz, G.R. No. 203878 (2020)

CARD civil-17
FRONT (shape): One spouse sells or encumbers a conjugal/community property without the written consent of the other spouse or court authority.
FRONT (trigger words): conjugal property sale, community property, without consent, void disposition, joint administration
BACK (provision): Art. 96 & 124, Family Code - Sale of Conjugal/Community Property without Consent
BACK (elements):
1. Property belongs to absolute community or conjugal partnership
2. One spouse disposes or encumbered the property
3. Done without the written consent of the other spouse or authority of the court
4. The transaction is void in its entirety (not merely voidable), but constitutes a continuing offer to the non-consenting spouse
BACK (common confusion): Art. 1390 (Voidable Contracts) :: A sale of conjugal property without the other spouse's consent is void under the Family Code (Art. 124), not voidable under the Civil Code (which was the old rule under the Civil Code Art. 166/173).
SOURCE: Family Code Arts. 96, 124; Bar 2018 Q8

CARD civil-18
FRONT (shape): A buyer purchases registered land relying solely on the face of the Torrens certificate of title, without knowledge of any defect.
FRONT (trigger words): mirror doctrine, Torrens title, innocent purchaser for value, registered land, face of the title
BACK (provision): Sec. 44, PD 1529 - Mirror Doctrine / Innocent Purchaser for Value
BACK (elements):
1. The land is registered under the Torrens System
2. The buyer purchases the property in good faith and for value
3. The buyer relies on the clean certificate of title
4. The buyer is not required to look beyond the face of the title, except when there are annotations or circumstances triggering suspicion
BACK (common confusion): Bad Faith Buyers :: If the buyer has actual knowledge of a prior claim or defect in the vendor's title, they cannot invoke the mirror doctrine; they are considered buyers in bad faith.
SOURCE: PD 1529 Sec. 44; Bar 2017 Q12

CARD civil-19
FRONT (shape): A marriage is celebrated without a valid marriage license, and it does not fall under any of the exceptional marriages exempt from the license requirement.
FRONT (trigger words): marriage license, absence of license, void marriage, essential requisition, exempt marriages
BACK (provision): Arts. 4 & 35(3), Family Code - Void Marriage due to Absence of License
BACK (elements):
1. Marriage celebrated between a man and a woman
2. No valid marriage license was issued prior to the marriage
3. The marriage does not qualify as an exempt marriage (e.g., cohabitation of 5+ years, marriage at point of death)
4. The marriage is void from inception (void ab initio)
BACK (common confusion): Art. 45 (Voidable Marriage due to Consent/Age) :: Absence of a formal requisite (like a license) makes the marriage void from inception under Art. 35. Defects in consent or age make the marriage voidable under Art. 45.
SOURCE: Family Code Arts. 4, 35(3); Bar 2019 Q5

CARD civil-20
FRONT (shape): A testator disposes of their entire estate by will, leaving nothing or less than the statutory share to their compulsory heirs.
FRONT (trigger words): legitime, compulsory heir, preterition, disinheritance, preterited heir, free portion
BACK (provision): Art. 886-887, Civil Code - Legitime of Compulsory Heirs
BACK (elements):
1. The testator has compulsory heirs (e.g. legitimate children, spouse)
2. The testator disposes of property by will or donation
3. The disposition deprives or impairs the legitime (reserved portion of the estate) of the compulsory heirs
4. The impaired heirs may demand completion or reduction of donations/legacies (remedy of inoficiosidad)
BACK (common confusion): Art. 854 (Preterition) :: Preterition is the total omission of a compulsory heir in the direct line in a will, which annuls the institution of heirs. Deprivation of legitime without total omission only entitles the heir to demand completion of legitime.
SOURCE: Civil Code Arts. 854, 886-887; Bar 2018 Q6

### DECOY PAIRS


DECOY decoy-1
SUBJECT: civil-law
SHAPE_A: shape-civil-1
SHAPE_B: shape-civil-6
SHARED_TRIGGER: forged deed
DISTINQUISHING_FACT: If the forged deed is used by a third party to sell the owner's land to a second buyer *without the owner's participation*, it is nullity under Art. 1409. If the **owner** is selling to A but B forges a deed to sell to C as well, and both are competing buyers, it is a double sale under Art. 1544 — the distinguishing fact is whether a valid sale by the **true owner** exists alongside the forged one.

DECOY decoy-2
SUBJECT: civil-law
SHAPE_A: shape-civil-2
SHAPE_B: shape-civil-5
SHARED_TRIGGER: damages negligence
DISTINQUISHING_FACT: In quasi-delict there is **no pre-existing contract** between the parties. In breach of contract, a contractual relation existed and the breach of that contract caused the damage. A single negligent act may give rise to both only if there is a contract AND a separate tort independent of the contractual breach.
