# Remedial Law — Shapes, Trigger Words, Flashcards
Generated from 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure (A.M. 19-10-20-SC), Rules on Evidence (A.M. 19-08-15-SC)

---

CARD rem-1
FRONT (shape): A plaintiff files a complaint in court, and the defendant argues that the court has no jurisdiction over the person or subject matter.
FRONT (trigger words): jurisdiction, venue, motion to dismiss, subject matter jurisdiction, personal jurisdiction, improper venue
BACK (provision): Rule 1 Secs. 1-2, Rule 9 Sec. 1, Rule 12 Sec. 1 — Jurisdiction and Venue
BACK (elements):
1. A complaint or initiatory pleading is filed in court
2. The court must have jurisdiction over the: (a) subject matter (conferred by law, cannot be waived), (b) person of the defendant (by service of summons or voluntary appearance)
3. Venue is the place of trial — may be agreed upon by the parties or fixed by the Rules
4. Grounds for motion to dismiss (Rule 16): lack of jurisdiction, improper venue, prescription, failure to state cause of action
5. Lack of subject matter jurisdiction may be raised at ANY TIME (even on appeal)
6. Improper venue must be raised in the FIRST responsive pleading or it is waived
BACK (common confusion): Jurisdiction vs. Venue :: Jurisdiction is the POWER of the court to hear a case — it is conferred by law and cannot be waived. Venue is the PLACE of trial — it can be waived and can be agreed upon by the parties. A court may have jurisdiction but be the wrong venue.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rules 1, 9, 12, 16; Bar 2019 Q13

CARD rem-2
FRONT (shape): A party files a civil case, and the opposing party fails to file an answer within the reglementary period.
FRONT (trigger words): default, failure to answer, declaration of default, judgment by default, answer, motion to lift
BACK (provision): Rule 9 Sec. 3, Rule 41 — Default
BACK (elements):
1. A complaint or initiatory pleading is filed
2. Summons is served on the defendant
3. The defendant fails to file an answer within the reglementary period (15-30 days from service)
4. The plaintiff may file a motion to declare the defendant in default
5. The court declares the defendant in default → plaintiff presents evidence ex parte
6. The court renders judgment based on plaintiff's evidence
7. The defaulted defendant may file a motion to lift the order of default within the period set by the court
8. The remedy from a default judgment is appeal
BACK (common confusion): Default vs. Dismissal :: Default applies when the DEFENDANT fails to answer — plaintiff wins if evidence is sufficient. Dismissal applies when the PLAINTIFF fails to prosecute — the defendant may win dismissal. Default does not automatically entitle the plaintiff to judgment; evidence must still be presented.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 9 Sec. 3, Rule 41

CARD rem-3
FRONT (shape): A party files a motion for summary judgment claiming there is no genuine issue of material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.
FRONT (trigger words): summary judgment, no genuine issue, material fact, immediate judgment, affidavit, motion
BACK (provision): Rule 36, Rule 35 — Summary Judgment
BACK (elements):
1. A civil action is pending
2. The moving party claims there is no genuine issue as to any material fact
3. The motion is supported by affidavits, depositions, admissions, or documents
4. The opposing party must present specific facts showing a genuine issue for trial
5. If no genuine issue exists, the court renders summary judgment for the movant
6. Summary judgment may be partial (Rule 36: judgment on some claims, the rest go to trial)
7. Summary judgment is NOT available when credibility of witnesses is in issue
BACK (common confusion): Summary Judgment vs. Judgment on the Pleadings :: Summary judgment considers EVIDENCE (affidavits, documents) to determine if there is a genuine factual issue. Judgment on the pleadings (Rule 34) considers ONLY THE PLEADINGS — the court decides based solely on the allegations. Summary judgment is granted when evidence shows no factual dispute.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rules 35-36

CARD rem-4
FRONT (shape): A party files a complaint, and the defendant files a counterclaim, cross-claim, or third-party complaint as part of the same action.
FRONT (trigger words): counterclaim, cross-claim, third-party complaint, compulsory counterclaim, permissive counterclaim, implender
BACK (provision): Rule 6 Secs. 6-9, Rule 12 — Counterclaims, Cross-Claims, and Third-Party Complaints
BACK (elements):
1. A complaint is filed in court
2. Compulsory counterclaim (Sec. 7): arises out of the SAME transaction — must be filed or it is barred forever
3. Permissive counterclaim (Sec. 7): independent of the plaintiff's claim — may be filed separately
4. Cross-claim (Sec. 8): claim against a CO-PARTY — arises out of the same transaction
5. Third-party complaint (Rule 12): defendant sues a THIRD PARTY who may be liable to the defendant for the plaintiff's claim
6. Counterclaims exceeding the jurisdictional amount of the court → additional docket fees
BACK (common confusion): Compulsory vs. Permissive Counterclaim :: A compulsory counterclaim arises from the SAME SUBJECT MATTER as the complaint — it MUST be filed in the same case or it is BARRED forever. A permissive counterclaim is an independent claim — it may be filed separately without being barred.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rules 6, 12; Bar 2020 Q8

CARD rem-5
FRONT (shape): A party appeals a judgment or final order to a higher court, questioning errors of fact or law.
FRONT (trigger words): appeal, final order, interlocutory order, notice of appeal, appeal bond, perfection of appeal
BACK (provision): Rule 41, 42, 43, 45 — Appeals from Judgments
BACK (elements):
1. A judgment or final order is rendered by a trial court
2. An interlocutory order (not final) cannot be appealed — remedy is certiorari
3. The appeal must be taken within the reglementary period (15 days from notice of judgment, extendable by 15 more days)
4. For RTC decisions: appeal to CA via ordinary appeal (Rule 41) or petition for review
5. For CA decisions: appeal to SC via petition for review on certiorari (Rule 45) — questions of LAW only
6. Perfection of appeal: filing notice of appeal + appeal bond + payment of docket fees
7. Failure to perfect within the period → judgment becomes final and executory
BACK (common confusion): Final vs. Interlocutory Order :: A final order DISPOSES of the case — it ends the litigation. An interlocutory order is issued during the trial (e.g. denial of a motion to dismiss, order granting summary judgment on a procedural matter). Only FINAL orders may be appealed; interlocutory orders may be reviewed only after final judgment.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rules 41-45; Bar 2018 Q13

CARD rem-6
FRONT (shape): A party seeks a preliminary injunction or TRO to prevent an act from being done before the case is fully heard.
FRONT (trigger words): injunction, TRO, temporary restraining order, preliminary injunction, irreparable injury, bond
BACK (provision): Rule 58 — Preliminary Injunction and TRO
BACK (elements):
1. There is a case filed or about to be filed
2. The applicant claims that an act is about to be done that would cause irreparable injury
3. For TRO (ex parte — 72 hours): applicant must show immediate, extreme urgency; must post a bond
4. For preliminary injunction (with notice): applicant must show clear legal right and irreparable injury; must post a bond
5. The court fixes the TRO period (max 72 hours, extendable by 20 days with notice)
6. The party enjoined may file a motion to dissolve the injunction/TRO
7. The bond answers for damages if the injunction was wrongfully issued
BACK (common confusion): TRO vs. Preliminary Injunction :: A TRO is a TEMPORARY measure (72 hours) issued WITHOUT notice in extreme urgency. A preliminary injunction is issued AFTER notice and hearing — it lasts until the case is decided. A TRO is intended to maintain the status quo until the court can hear the application for preliminary injunction.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 58

CARD rem-7
FRONT (shape): A party moves for a new trial or to set aside a judgment claiming errors or newly discovered evidence.
FRONT (trigger words): new trial, reconsideration, newly discovered evidence, judgment notwithstanding verdict, set aside judgment
BACK (provision): Rule 37 — New Trial and Reconsideration
BACK (elements):
1. A judgment is rendered
2. Grounds for new trial (Sec. 1): (a) fraud, accident, mistake, or excusable negligence, (b) newly discovered evidence that could not have been discovered earlier
3. Grounds for reconsideration: errors of law or fact in the judgment — no new evidence
4. The motion must be filed within 15 days from notice of judgment
5. For newly discovered evidence: the movant must show: (a) it was discovered after trial, (b) it could not have been discovered earlier with due diligence, (c) it would probably alter the result
6. Denial of the motion → the movant may appeal the original judgment
BACK (common confusion): New Trial vs. Reconsideration :: New Trial opens the case for RE-EXAMINATION of evidence — the court rehears the case and reverses, modifies, or affirms the judgment. Reconsideration asks the court to RE-EXAMINE the legal basis — no new evidence is presented. New trial is for factual errors; reconsideration is for legal errors.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 37

CARD rem-8
FRONT (shape): A party offers evidence at trial, and the opposing party objects on grounds of hearsay, relevance, or the best evidence rule.
FRONT (trigger words): hearsay, objection, evidence, admissibility, relevance, best evidence, testimony
BACK (provision): Rule 128-134 (Rules on Evidence) — Admissibility of Evidence
BACK (elements):
1. A party offers evidence (documentary, object, or testimonial)
2. The evidence must be relevant to the issue and not excluded by law (Rule 128 Sec. 3)
3. Hearsay rule (Rule 130 Sec. 37): a statement other than one made by the declarant while testifying is inadmissible to prove the truth of the matter asserted — EXCEPTIONS apply
4. Best evidence rule (Rule 130 Sec. 3): the original document is required to prove its contents — EXCEPTIONS apply when the original is unavailable through no fault of the offering party
5. The opposing party must make a TIMELY objection (Rule 132 Sec. 36) — otherwise the objection is waived
6. The court rules on the objection: sustain (exclude) or overrule (admit)
BACK (common confusion): Objection vs. Motion to Strike :: An objection is made BEFORE the evidence is admitted (during a question or the offering of a document). A motion to strike is made after inadmissible evidence has been admitted — it asks the court to disregard it. A late objection may be treated as a motion to strike.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules on Evidence, Rules 128-134; Bar 2021 Q12

CARD rem-9
FRONT (shape): A party takes the deposition of a witness or requests documents from the opposing party before trial.
FRONT (trigger words): deposition, discovery, interrogatories, request for admission, production of documents, pretrial
BACK (provision): Rule 23-28, Rule 18 — Discovery Procedures and Pre-Trial
BACK (elements):
1. A case is pending
2. Discovery methods: (a) depositions (Rule 23), (b) interrogatories to parties (Rule 25), (c) request for admission (Rule 26), (d) production of documents (Rule 27), (e) physical/mental examination (Rule 28)
3. Pre-trial (Rule 18): the court issues a pre-trial order after the pre-trial conference
4. Pre-trial includes: marking of evidence, stipulation of facts, issues to be tried, witness list
5. Failure to comply with discovery orders → the court may impose sanctions (striking of pleadings, dismissal)
6. Request for admission: if the requested party fails to respond within 15 days, the matters are deemed ADMITTED
BACK (common confusion): Request for Admission vs. Interrogatories :: Request for admission asks the party to ADMIT or DENY specific facts — failure to respond = deemed admission. Interrogatories ask for WRITTEN ANSWERS under oath — no automatic admission for failure to respond (though sanctions may apply).
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rules 18, 23-28

CARD rem-10
FRONT (shape): A party seeks to enforce a judgment through execution, claiming the judgment debtor has assets that may be levied upon.
FRONT (trigger words): execution, judgment, levy, garnishment, writ of execution, satisfaction of judgment, property levy
BACK (provision): Rule 39 — Execution of Judgments
BACK (elements):
1. A final and executory judgment exists
2. The prevailing party files a motion for execution (or the court issues it motu proprio after 5 years)
3. The writ of execution is issued by the court of origin
4. The sheriff executes by: (a) levy on personal property (Rule 39 Secs. 9-10), (b) garnishment of debts/credits (Sec. 11), (c) levy on real property (Secs. 14-19)
5. If the judgment is NOT FULLY SATISFIED within 5 years, the judgment may be revived by an action to revive the judgment within 10 years
6. Exempt properties are not subject to execution (family home, necessary clothing, tools of the trade)
BACK (common confusion): Levy vs. Garnishment :: Levy applies to the judgment debtor's TANGIBLE property (personal or real) — the sheriff physically takes or records a lien. Garnishment applies to the debtor's RIGHTS or CREDITS (bank accounts, receivables) — a third party holding the debtor's property is ordered to deliver it to the sheriff.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 39; Bar 2022 Q6

CARD rem-11
FRONT (shape): A person is charged with a crime, and the court determines whether probable cause exists to issue a warrant of arrest or hold the accused for trial.
FRONT (trigger words): probable cause, preliminary investigation, information, arrest warrant, inquest, filing of case
BACK (provision): Rule 110-112 (Rules of Criminal Procedure) — Prosecution of Offenses
BACK (elements):
1. A complaint is filed with the prosecutor or the court
2. Preliminary investigation is conducted for offenses with penalty of at least 4 years and 2 months
3. The investigating prosecutor determines if there is PROBABLE CAUSE to file an Information in court
4. For warrantless arrests: inquest proceeds immediately (Article 125 RPC)
5. If probable cause is found: the Information is filed in the proper court
6. The court independently finds probable cause to issue a warrant of arrest (or to hold for trial if no warrant required)
7. The accused must be arraigned within 30 days from the court's finding of probable cause (or from arrest)
BACK (common confusion): Preliminary Investigation vs. Inquest :: Preliminary investigation is a FULL PROCEEDING with notice and hearing — applies when the accused is LAWFULLY ARRESTED with a warrant. Inquest is a SUMMARY proceeding — applies to WARRANTLESS arrests where the accused is immediately brought before the prosecutor.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Criminal Procedure Rules 110-112; Bar 2017 Q10

CARD rem-12
FRONT (shape): A party seeks a writ of certiorari to challenge a tribunal's grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction.
FRONT (trigger words): certiorari, grave abuse discretion, Rule 65, extraordinary remedy, no appeal, jurisdiction
BACK (provision): Rule 65 — Certiorari, Prohibition, Mandamus
BACK (elements):
1. A tribunal, board, or officer exercising judicial or quasi-judicial functions commits grave abuse of discretion
2. There is NO appeal or any other plain, speedy, and adequate remedy in the ordinary course of law
3. Certiorari (Rule 65 Sec. 1): corrects errors of JURISDICTION or GRAVE ABUSE of discretion
4. Prohibition (Sec. 2): orders a lower court to REFRAIN from acting beyond its jurisdiction
5. Mandamus (Sec. 3): commands a tribunal to perform a MINISTERIAL duty (not discretionary)
6. The petition must be filed within 60 days from notice of the assailed judgment/order
7. The court may issue a TEMPORARY RESTRAINING ORDER (TRO) or writ of preliminary injunction pending the petition
BACK (common confusion): Rule 65 Certiorari vs. Ordinary Appeal (Rule 41-45) :: Ordinary appeal reviews ERRORS OF FACT AND LAW in a final judgment. Rule 65 certiorari reviews GRAVE ABUSE OF DISCRETION that goes to JURISDICTION. Certiorari is an extraordinary remedy — it is available only when there is NO appeal.
SOURCE: 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 65; Bar 2020 Q6
