# Taxation — Shapes, Trigger Words, Flashcards
Generated from RA 8424 (National Internal Revenue Code)

---

CARD tax-1
FRONT (shape): A taxpayer receives income from compensation, business, or profession, and the BIR assesses a deficiency tax for non-payment or underpayment.
FRONT (trigger words): income tax, gross income, taxable income, deductions, deficiency assessment, return
BACK (provision): Secs. 22-36, 50-57 (NIRC) - Income Tax: Gross Income, Deductions, and Assessment
BACK (elements):
1. The taxpayer has gross income from: (a) compensation, (b) business/profession, (c) passive income, (d) capital gains
2. Gross income includes all income from whatever source derived (Sec. 32) unless expressly exempted
3. Deductions are allowed for: (a) business expenses (Sec. 34), (b) personal and additional exemptions (Sec. 35)
4. Taxable income = gross income - allowable deductions
5. The BIR may issue a deficiency assessment if the tax return understates income or overstates deductions
6. The taxpayer has the right to protest the assessment within 30 days
BACK (common confusion): Gross Income vs. Gross Revenue :: Gross income includes ALL income from all sources. Gross revenue only includes income from the taxpayer's main business activity. Certain items (e.g. gifts, inheritance) may not be income subject to tax. Income = "flow of wealth" — any increase in net worth.
SOURCE: NIRC Secs. 22-36; Bar 2018 Q12

CARD tax-2
FRONT (shape): A seller of goods or services collects VAT from customers, and the BIR questions the proper remittance or the applicability of VAT exemption.
FRONT (trigger words): VAT, value-added tax, output tax, input tax, VAT exemption, invoice, zero-rated
BACK (provision): Secs. 105-115 (NIRC) - Value-Added Tax
BACK (elements):
1. The taxpayer is engaged in trade or business
2. The taxpayer's gross sales/receipts exceed P3,000,000 (VAT threshold) (Sec. 109)
3. The taxpayer must register as a VAT taxpayer
4. Output VAT is charged on every sale of goods/properties/services (currently 12%)
5. Input VAT on purchases may be credited against output VAT
6. If input VAT exceeds output VAT: the excess may be carried over as tax credit
7. VAT-exempt transactions: exempt by law (e.g. agricultural products, education, health services)
BACK (common confusion): VAT-Exempt vs. Zero-Rated :: VAT-exempt means NO VAT is charged and NO input VAT credit is allowed. Zero-rated means 0% VAT is charged but input VAT CREDIT is allowed (exporter makes supplies for foreign consumption). Zero-rated is more favorable — the taxpayer gets refund of input VAT.
SOURCE: NIRC Secs. 105-115; Bar 2020 Q4

CARD tax-3
FRONT (shape): The BIR conducts an audit or investigation of a taxpayer and issues an assessment notice, and the taxpayer disputes the assessment through administrative or judicial remedies.
FRONT (trigger words): BIR assessment, tax audit, Letter of Authority, FLAG, deficiency notice, protest, CTA
BACK (provision): Secs. 228-229, 234-235 (NIRC) - Tax Assessment, Protest, and Appeal
BACK (elements):
1. The BIR issues a Letter of Authority (LOA) authorizing a tax audit
2. After audit, the BIR issues a Preliminary Assessment Notice (PAN) — taxpayer may reply
3. The BIR issues a Formal Letter of Demand and Final Assessment Notice (FLDA/FAN)
4. The taxpayer may protest within 30 days from receipt of FAN
5. If protested and denied, the taxpayer may appeal to the Court of Tax Appeals (CTA) within 30 days
6. The CTA has exclusive appellate jurisdiction over tax cases
7. Prescriptive period: BIR must assess within 3 years from filing of return, or 10 years if the return is false or no return is filed
BACK (common confusion): 3-Year vs. 10-Year Prescriptive Period :: The 3-year period applies when a tax RETURN IS FILED. The 10-year period applies when there is NO RETURN FILED or a FALSE/RETURN. If the BIR assesses after the prescriptive period without showing fraud, the assessment is void.
SOURCE: NIRC Secs. 228-229, 234-235; Bar 2019 Q12

CARD tax-4
FRONT (shape): A taxpayer sells real property or shares of stock and is subject to capital gains tax or donor's tax on the transfer.
FRONT (trigger words): capital gains tax, CGT, real property, shares of stock, sale, transfer, donor's tax
BACK (provision): Secs. 24(D), 27(D), 84-99 (NIRC) - Capital Gains and Donor's Tax
BACK (elements):
1. There is a sale, exchange, or other disposition of a capital asset
2. For real property classified as capital asset: 6% CGT on the gross selling price or fair market value, whichever is higher
3. For shares of stock not traded on the stock exchange: 15% CGT on net capital gain
4. For shares traded on the stock exchange: 0.1% stock transaction tax (not CGT)
5. For donor's tax: any transfer of property without consideration or with inadequate consideration
6. Donor's tax rates: graduated rates from 0% to 30% depending on the total gifts (Sec. 84-99)
BACK (common confusion): CGT vs. Ordinary Income Tax :: CGT is a FINAL tax on the GAIN from selling capital assets — it is not included in the ordinary income tax return. If the property sold is classified as ORDINARY asset (stock-in-trade, real property used in business), the gain is subject to ordinary income tax rates, not CGT.
SOURCE: NIRC Secs. 24(D), 27(D), 84-99; Bar 2021 Q4

CARD tax-5
FRONT (shape): A person dies and leaves an estate, and the estate or heirs are required to pay estate tax before transferring title to the heirs.
FRONT (trigger words): estate tax, death, inheritance, estate settlement, executor, BIR clearance, eST filing
BACK (provision): Secs. 84-99, 140-147 (NIRC) - Estate Tax
BACK (elements):
1. A person dies leaving an estate (real or personal property)
2. The estate includes all properties of the decedent at the time of death
3. Estate tax is imposed on the NET estate (gross estate minus allowable deductions)
4. Allowable deductions: funeral expenses (up to P200,000), share of surviving spouse, medical expenses, standard deduction of P5,000,000 (RA 11534/CITIRA)
5. The estate tax return must be filed within 1 year from death (extendable up to 30 years with bond)
6. The estate tax clearance is required before transferring properties to the heirs
BACK (common confusion): Estate Tax vs. Donor's Tax :: Estate tax is imposed on property PASSING at death — tax on the privilege to transmit. Donor's tax is imposed on INTER VIVOS transfers (during life) — tax on the privilege to give. Both are excise taxes on the transfer, not on the property itself.
SOURCE: NIRC Secs. 84-99, 140-147; RA 11534

CARD tax-6
FRONT (shape): A person or business imports goods into the Philippines, and customs duties and taxes are assessed by the Bureau of Customs.
FRONT (trigger words): customs duties, importation, tariff, Bureau of Customs, valuation, classification, safeguard
BACK (provision): RA 10863 (CMTA) — Customs Modernization and Tariff Act; NIRC Secs. 107(A)
BACK (elements):
1. Goods are imported into the Philippine territory
2. The goods are subject to customs duties based on their tariff classification (AHTN)
3. Valuation: transaction value method (price actually paid or payable) — if unavailable, sequential methods apply
4. Dutiable value = CIF value (Cost + Insurance + Freight)
5. Imported goods are also subject to VAT (12% of total landed cost) and other taxes
6. The importer must file an import entry and pay duties/taxes before release
BACK (common confusion): Dutiable Value vs. Total Landed Cost :: Dutiable value is the CIF value — cost, insurance, and freight. Total landed cost = CIF + customs duty + other charges. VAT is computed on the total landed cost, not just the CIF value. So VAT = 12% × (CIF + duty + other charges).
SOURCE: RA 10863 (CMTA); NIRC Sec. 107(A)

CARD tax-7
FRONT (shape): A taxpayer fails to pay the correct tax due to negligence or fraud, and the BIR imposes penalties, surcharges, and interest.
FRONT (trigger words): tax penalty, surcharge, interest, compromise, negligence, fraud, tax delinquency
BACK (provision): Sec. 248-252 (NIRC) - Penalties, Surcharges, and Interest
BACK (elements):
1. A taxpayer fails to file a return, pays less than the correct amount, or files a false return
2. For negligence (no fraud): 50% surcharge on the deficiency tax
3. For willful neglect/fraud: 100% surcharge on the deficiency tax
4. Interest: 20% per annum (or current legal rate) on any unpaid tax (Sec. 249)
5. Compromise penalty: BIR may compromise payment of penalties for minor violations (Sec. 204)
6. The BIR may abate or cancel a tax liability when: (a) the assessment is excessive, (b) the taxpayer is financially incapable
BACK (common confusion): Civil Penalties vs. Criminal Penalties :: Civil penalties (surcharges, interest under Secs. 248-252) are imposed for non-compliance — they are civil in nature. Criminal penalties (imprisonment under Sec. 254-265) require WILLFUL intent to evade tax. The same act can give rise to both civil and criminal liability.
SOURCE: NIRC Secs. 248-252; Bar 2022 Q9

CARD tax-8
FRONT (shape): A taxpayer claims that a specific income is exempt from taxation under the Tax Code or a special law, and the BIR disputes the exemption.
FRONT (trigger words): tax exemption, exclusion, deduction, exempt income, tax treaty, interpretation
BACK (provision): Secs. 29-32 (NIRC) - Exclusions from Gross Income and Tax Exemptions
BACK (elements):
1. The taxpayer receives income or engages in a transaction
2. The taxpayer claims exemption from tax under a specific provision of law
3. Items excluded from gross income (Sec. 32): life insurance proceeds, gifts/inheritance, compensation for injuries, retirement benefits (within limits)
4. Tax exemptions are strictly construed against the taxpayer and liberally in favor of the government
5. For tax treaties: treaty provisions may override the NIRC (subject to reciprocity)
6. The taxpayer has the burden of proving entitlement to the exemption
BACK (common confusion): Exclusion vs. Deduction :: Exclusion means the item is NEVER included in gross income — it is not taxable at all. Deduction means the item is included in gross income but subtracted to arrive at taxable income. Exclusions (Sec. 32) are more favorable than deductions.
SOURCE: NIRC Secs. 29-32
